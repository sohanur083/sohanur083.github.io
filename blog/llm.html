Inside an LLM: tokens, attention, hallucinations

An LLM doesn't read words. It reads tokens, moves them through attention, and predicts the next token. That's it. Everything else — reasoning, creativity, and yes, hallucination — is emergent from this loop.

1. Words aren't atomic — tokens are

Before an LLM sees your prompt, it's chopped into tokens: sub-word units. A common word like cat is one token. A rare word like hallucination might split into halluc + ##ination. Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) and WordPiece are the two dominant schemes.

Why it matters: GPT-4 costs per token. A 2,000-word prompt is ~3,000 tokens, not 2,000. Non-English languages often tokenise worse (more tokens per concept), which is why multilingual prompts burn context faster.

2. Attention: how context becomes meaning

Every token asks: "which other tokens in the context should I listen to?" That weight matrix — who attends to whom, how much — is the attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). In "The doctor said the patient needs rest," the word patient attends strongly to doctor, because their relationship carries meaning.

✦ Attention Heatmap (toy example) row = query, col = key · darker = more attention

Notice how patient attends heavily to doctor and needs — the model binds the entity to its relevant context.

Transformers stack many attention heads in many layers. Each head learns a different kind of dependency: coreference, syntax, topic, sentiment, and things we still can't name. That's why interpretability research (my work!) is hard.

3. Why LLMs hallucinate

LLMs are trained to maximise the probability of the next token given prior tokens. They are not trained to check whether their output is true. As a result:

  • Intrinsic hallucination — output contradicts the input prompt.
  • Extrinsic hallucination — output contradicts world knowledge.
  • Confabulation — model invents plausible-but-fake citations, names, URLs.

In healthcare, where I work, this is dangerous. Our GenAI4SCH paper built a prompt-engineering framework that extracts causal relational rules from LLM outputs in medical QA — giving us something concrete to verify the model against, rather than trusting free-form generation.

4. Three patterns that reduce hallucination

a) Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Ground the model in a trusted knowledge base. Retrieve relevant documents first, stuff them into the prompt, then generate. The model now has evidence to cite instead of hallucinating.

b) Chain-of-verification / NLI judges

After generating, a second model (or the same model, second pass) checks: does this statement logically follow from the source? This is the core of our AAAI 2026 submission — using Natural Language Inference (NLI) judges to score summary faithfulness without ground truth.

c) Fine-tuning with preference data (RLHF/DPO)

Collect pairs of (chosen, rejected) outputs and train the model to prefer the chosen ones. This is how ChatGPT got polite, and how Constitutional AI makes Claude cautious. Still doesn't eliminate hallucination — just reduces it.

5. What you should take away

  • LLMs are next-token predictors. Brilliant at plausibility, indifferent to truth.
  • Attention is why they feel context-aware — but each head is a black box.
  • For high-stakes domains (medicine, law, finance): never deploy raw LLM output. Always add retrieval, verification, or human review.
