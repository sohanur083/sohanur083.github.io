#04 · Explainable AI

Opening the black box

A model that says "this patient is high-risk" is useless to a doctor who can't tell why. Explainable AI (XAI) is the set of tools that make models account for their decisions — the core of trustworthy AI.

Why XAI matters (especially in medicine)

Regulation requires it: the EU AI Act and FDA guidelines on medical AI both demand human-understandable explanations. Ethics demands it: you cannot dispute a diagnosis you can't understand. And science demands it: if we can't explain why a model works, we can't trust it on new data.

In my lab, when we build LLMs for medical question answering, we pair every prediction with a token-level explanation using Shapley values and LIME. Below is a simplified risk model you can probe yourself.

The three XAI workhorses

1. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)

Borrowed from game theory (Shapley, 1953). For each feature, compute its average marginal contribution across all possible orderings of the features. Result: a number that says "this feature added +0.23 to the prediction." Mathematically principled, slow to compute exactly, but well-approximated.

We use shap.Explainer on our medical LLMs to identify which tokens in the question / context pushed the model toward its answer.

2. LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations)

For a single prediction, LIME builds a simple linear model (logistic regression, typically) that mimics the black-box model's behaviour in the neighbourhood of that prediction. The coefficients of the linear model are your explanation.

Cheaper than SHAP. Less principled, more hackable. Works on any model — even a model you only have API access to.

3. Attention analysis

For transformer models, inspect the attention weights directly (we did this in the LLM post). Caveat: attention is not explanation (Jain & Wallace, 2019). High attention doesn't mean high causal importance. Use it as a hint, not a proof.

"Attention is not explanation. But it is a hypothesis generator." — I quote this line in every talk I give.

Local vs global explanations

Local: why did the model predict this outcome for this patient? (SHAP, LIME, counterfactuals.)

Global: what does the model learn in general? (Feature importance across the test set, decision tree surrogates, concept activation vectors.)

In medicine we need both. Local explanations justify one prediction to a clinician. Global explanations let regulators audit the model's behaviour.

The honest limits of XAI

  • Explanations can lie. SHAP explains the model, not reality. If the model learned a spurious correlation (e.g. "pneumonia patients recover faster" because the sickest get admitted to ICU faster), SHAP will faithfully explain the spurious pattern.
  • Humans over-trust explanations. Showing any explanation, even a bad one, raises user trust more than it should (Bansal et al., 2021).
  • No method works for all models. SHAP is brittle for RNNs, LIME is noisy for high-dimensional inputs, attention-based methods don't apply to non-transformers.

Where the field is going

My own bet: structured, verifiable explanations. Instead of "here are 10 important tokens," make the model output a causal rule, a citation, a logical proof. Something that can be checked by a second system. That's the direction of our work on causal relational rules for medical QA, and the multi-agent evaluation framework under review at AAAI 2026.

AI that explains itself is good. AI that explains itself in a way another AI (or human) can verify is the real goal. That's trustworthy AI.
