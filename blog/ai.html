#01 · AI Fundamentals

What is AI, really?

"Artificial intelligence" is thrown around like magic. Strip the marketing away and it's one idea: a machine that maps inputs to outputs using rules learned from data. Here's how we got from symbolic rules to ChatGPT — in 6 minutes.

1. The symbolic era (1950s – 1980s)

Early AI was hand-written rules. You wanted a chess program? A human encoded every opening, every strategy, every heuristic. If you wanted medical diagnosis? Same — a team of doctors wrote thousands of IF symptom THEN disease rules (see MYCIN, 1972).

Works great for narrow, well-defined problems. Fails spectacularly the moment reality is messy: handwriting, speech, translation, vision. The rules explode combinatorially.

"Can a machine think?" — Alan Turing, 1950. Still the best question in the field.

2. The statistical turn (1990s – 2010s)

Instead of humans writing rules, we give the machine data and let it fit a function. Spam filter? Show it 10,000 spam emails + 10,000 ham emails. The model learns which word-combinations signal spam. We stopped asking "what's the rule?" and started asking "what does the data say?"

This is where you get logistic regression, SVMs, decision trees, random forests. The core primitive most people still don't grok: the perceptron. One neuron. A weighted sum of inputs plus a bias, squashed through a step. That's it.

The perceptron's rule: if w₁·x + w₂·y + b > 0 → class A, else class B. That single line of math is the entire core of deep learning — we just stack millions of them together.

3. The deep learning explosion (2012 – now)

2012: AlexNet slaughtered the ImageNet competition using a convolutional neural network and two GPUs. Everything changed. Stack enough perceptrons, train on enough data, and you get:

  • CNNs — see images (ImageNet, face recognition, medical imaging)
  • RNNs / LSTMs — model sequences (translation, speech)
  • Transformers — attend to context (GPT, BERT, Claude, Gemini)

The scary part: nobody explicitly taught GPT grammar. It learned it. Nobody explicitly taught it reasoning. It emerges from predicting the next token over trillions of words. That's both exhilarating and terrifying — which is why my research focuses on making sure these models don't confidently lie.

4. What AI is not

AI is not magic. It's not sentient. It's not "understanding" in the human sense. It's a statistical pattern matcher trained on human output. Powerful, genuinely useful, but bounded by its training data. When you see a model confidently cite a fake paper, that's not a bug — it's the system working exactly as designed: generating plausible text, not true text.

5. Where to go next

The perceptron above is 2D. Real networks have billions of parameters across hundreds of layers. Same idea, more scale. In the next post we build up to "how does a model actually learn those weights?" — the magic trick is gradient descent, and you can watch it happen live.
