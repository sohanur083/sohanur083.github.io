#06 · Fine-Tuning

Fine-tuning LLMs efficiently

A 70B model has 140 GB of weights. Full fine-tuning needs ~700 GB of VRAM. Most of us don't have that. This is how we actually fine-tune large models without renting a cluster — and when each method is worth the trade-off.

What fine-tuning actually does

You take a pre-trained model (Llama, Mistral, Qwen) and continue training it on your data for a narrower objective. The base model already understands language; you're teaching it to follow your instructions, adopt your style, or know your domain.

Three modes you'll hear about:

  1. Continued pre-training — more next-token prediction on domain text (medical notes, legal contracts). Adjusts world knowledge.
  2. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) — pairs of (prompt, desired response). Teaches format + task.
  3. Preference tuning (RLHF, DPO) — pairs of (chosen, rejected). Teaches taste + safety.

Full fine-tuning: the expensive baseline

Update every weight in the model. You need:

  • Weights in fp16: 2 bytes × N params
  • Gradients: same size as weights
  • Adam optimiser state: 2 × fp32 copy = 8 bytes per param
  • Activations: scales with batch size × sequence length

For a 7B model: 14 GB + 14 GB + 56 GB ≈ ~84 GB minimum, plus activations. One A100 80GB is tight. Full fine-tune of a 70B? You need 8× A100 with FSDP or DeepSpeed.

It works. It's also overkill for almost every use case. Your fine-tuning data is usually a few thousand examples. You don't actually need to nudge all 7 billion parameters.

LoRA — the killer idea

Hu et al., 2021. Observation: the update to a big weight matrix during fine-tuning is approximately low-rank. So don't learn the full update. Learn a rank-r factorisation of it. 

W_new = W_frozen + ΔW
ΔW = A · B    where A is d×r, B is r×d,  r ≪ d

You freeze the original weights and only train A and B. For d=4096 and r=8, you go from ~16M parameters per matrix down to ~65K. ~0.1% of the original parameter count.

  • 3–10× less VRAM
  • Trains 2–3× faster
  • Final quality: often within 1% of full fine-tune on instruction-tuning tasks
  • Adapter file is tiny (~50–200 MB) — ship it separately from the base model

QLoRA — push it even further

Dettmers et al., 2023. Add one trick to LoRA: quantise the frozen base model to 4-bit (NF4 format). You don't need fp16 precision on weights you're not updating.

Result: you can fine-tune a 65B model on a single 48 GB GPU. The paper's Guanaco model beat ChatGPT on the Vicuna benchmark, fine-tuned in 24 hours on one consumer GPU.

The full PEFT zoo

LoRA is the most popular but not the only approach. The Hugging Face peft library ships them all:

  • LoRA — low-rank adapter matrices. The default choice.
  • QLoRA — LoRA + 4-bit quantised base. For huge models on one GPU.
  • AdaLoRA — adaptively allocates rank per layer. Slight quality boost.
  • LoRA-FA — freeze A, only train B. Half the adapter memory.
  • Prefix tuning — prepend a small learned "virtual prompt" vector. Tiny params, weaker results.
  • P-tuning / Prompt tuning — learn a soft prompt embedding only. Works for classification, struggles for generation.
  • IA³ — learn per-channel scaling factors for attention + FFN. Even fewer params than LoRA.
  • DoRA — decomposes weights into magnitude + direction, only learns direction. Often beats LoRA at same rank.

Instruction tuning vs preference tuning

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Dataset is pairs: (prompt, good_answer). Standard cross-entropy loss. Teaches the model what format you want and what domain you care about. Start here. 1K–50K examples goes a long way.

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)

Three stages: (1) SFT, (2) train a reward model on human preference pairs, (3) use PPO to optimise the LLM against the reward model. Powerful, unstable, expensive. How ChatGPT was built.

DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)

Rafailov et al., 2023. Skip the reward model. Optimise the LLM directly on preference pairs with a clever closed-form loss. Same quality as RLHF, way simpler to run. This is what most open-source projects use now (Zephyr, Mistral Instruct, Nous-Hermes).

ORPO / KTO / SimPO

Further simplifications of DPO. ORPO (Odds Ratio Preference Optimization) combines SFT and preference tuning in one step. Worth trying if you're optimising training budget.

Best-practice checklist (my personal one)

  1. Do you actually need to fine-tune? Try prompt engineering + few-shot first. Then RAG. Fine-tune only if those plateau.
  2. Data quality > data quantity. 1K clean examples beat 100K noisy ones. Always.
  3. Default to QLoRA. Unless you have a good reason for full FT. r=16, alpha=32, dropout=0.05 is a solid starting point.
  4. Target modules matter. For Llama, apply LoRA to q_proj, k_proj, v_proj, o_proj, gate_proj, up_proj, down_proj — all the projections, not just attention.
  5. Eval before you ship. Hold out a test set. Compare against the base model. If fine-tuning made it worse on anything, figure out why.
  6. Stop early. Most fine-tunes overfit after 2–3 epochs on small datasets. Use validation loss, not train loss.
  7. Save adapters, not merged weights — until you're ready to deploy. Adapters are tiny, composable, and you can try many.
"The 3AM version of this project" test: can you re-run your fine-tune from scratch at 3AM after waking up confused? If yes, your config is right. If it's a fragile stack of custom scripts, refactor.

Tools you'll actually use

  • Hugging Face transformers + peft + trl — the standard stack
  • Axolotl — YAML-driven fine-tuning framework, handles all the boilerplate
  • Unsloth — optimised kernels, 2× faster LoRA fine-tuning on consumer GPUs
  • LLaMA-Factory — GUI + CLI for fine-tuning, good for non-coders
  • Weights & Biases / MLflow — always log your runs. Always.

Mistakes I've watched people make

  • Fine-tuning a chat model on plain text completions → broke the chat template. Always keep the instruction format consistent.
  • Using learning rate 5e-5 (standard for BERT) on a Llama-LoRA. Too low. LoRA typically wants 1e-4 to 3e-4.
  • Mixing fp16 optimiser states on bf16 trained models. Use bf16 end-to-end on Ampere+ GPUs.
  • Fine-tuning on their test set by accident. Always split by source, not row.

Bottom line

In 2026, fine-tuning is mostly solved at the small scale. QLoRA on a single A100 will get you 95% of the way for most tasks. The hard part is no longer compute — it's data curation, evaluation, and deployment. Spend your time there.
